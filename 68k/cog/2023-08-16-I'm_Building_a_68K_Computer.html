I'm Building a 68k Computer

Or rather, I did, as you may have seen on Mastodon. I'll be going through my brief little journey getting it running as a series of short posts.

The Motorola 68000 is a bit of a frontier to me. Up to this point, I didn't know much about the architecture, but love many of the machines that used it, like the Amiga, Sharp X68000, Sega Mega Drive, among many others.

Having programmed the NES, Game Boy, DOS, PlayStation 1, and modern computers, there was a distinct hole where the 68000, 8086, and 65816 should be. I already know enough about the latter two to know that I'm not interested at this time ^^. However, the more I learned about the architecture and instruction set of the 68000, I knew I wanted it to be the real representative of my knowledge about this era.

The 16-bit era has a particular feeling that is difficult to place. The sound chips began to blossom with samples and FM synthesis, whilst still remaining primitive enough to maintain a distinct identity. The same goes for graphics. Once the 32-bit era arrived, with 3D graphics and streaming CD-quality audio, everything very quickly became just another PC lacking a unique experience or identity. This was especially stark for consoles after the PlayStation 2, in my humble opinion.

The 68000 lies in a sweet spot. With 8 data registers, 8 address registers, and an instruction set allowing many operations on any registers, there is a modest beauty and reasonable complexity to it. There is even a sensible default ABI for C programs. It has a lot going for it. Whilst I tend to favor RISC architectures for their sheer elegance and simplicity, there is much to admire about the 68000.
